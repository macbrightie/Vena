# Lead Magnets & Conversion Assets Guide

*Built from the conversion-bucket and profile-funnel references in the LinkedIn System for 2026 transcripts (Distinct Diva) and the lead-magnet idea in Matthew Leave's hook session. Structured for an AI system to generate and place lead magnets, not just describe them.*

## A note on scope

The source documents don't contain a dedicated lead-magnet framework — "lead magnet" shows up as one tactic inside the Conversion bucket and the profile funnel. This guide pulls every reference to it together into one usable system, and is explicit below about which parts are directly stated in the source versus a direct, logical extension of it (clearly marked).

---

## 1. Where lead magnets live in the system

The source describes lead magnets as living in three places, working together rather than as one standalone tactic:

1. **The Featured section on your profile.** Its stated job is to move people one step closer into your world — somewhere you own the relationship, such as a newsletter sign-up, booking link, or lead magnet. A post only belongs in Featured if it's genuinely exceptional; a booking link or newsletter always takes priority over a post.
2. **The custom CTA button on your profile.** LinkedIn lets you add a button directly on your profile. Most people leave it blank or link to their homepage — the source calls this "free real estate" that should link to a booking page, a download, or a newsletter instead.
3. **Conversion-bucket posts themselves.** Where the lead magnet is actually offered and delivered, usually through a comment-or-DM mechanic (see Section 3).

> "LinkedIn owns your followers. You don't." — the stated reason every lead magnet should pull people off-platform into something you own.

---

## 2. Why lead magnets only work after trust is built

Conversion content — including lead magnets — only works once Growth and Authority content (see the Post Frameworks guide, Section D) have already done their job. The source is direct: "you can't convert people who don't trust you yet." That means a lead magnet should never be the first thing a new follower sees from you; it should appear after they've already seen you demonstrate expertise (Authority) or seen a take that made them stop and think (Growth).

Practical sequencing rule for an AI system building a content calendar: never schedule a Conversion/lead-magnet post as someone's likely first touchpoint. Place it after a run of Growth and Authority posts in the same week or two, per the bucket ratio in the Post Frameworks guide.

---

## 3. How a lead magnet gets offered inside a post

The source gives three concrete delivery mechanics, all tied to the Conversion and Authority buckets:

- Offering a free resource in exchange for a comment or DM.
- "DM me for the template" / "comment X for the full framework" — technically an Authority-bucket CTA, but functionally the delivery mechanism for a lead magnet, since it's the trigger that gets the resource into someone's hands.
- A link placed under your name rather than inside the post body — used because LinkedIn suppresses the reach of posts with outbound links in the main text.

Other Conversion-bucket examples mentioned that aren't lead magnets but sit in the same bucket and use the same sequencing logic: sharing that you're opening up spots for new clients, and promoting a newsletter or a new feature within your product.

---

## 4. The core principle: the lead magnet should fulfill the hook's exact promise

This comes directly from Matthew Leave's hook session. While iterating on a hook built around "leaked Diary of a CEO content secrets," he notes the natural next step: turn that exact promise into an actual deliverable — in his words, a short video "sharing the preview of how this can happen."

> "I might make this into a lead magnet of some sort... the lead magnet might just be me doing a video of sharing the preview of how this can happen." — Matthew Leave

The implication for an AI system: don't design lead magnets independently of your best-performing hooks. When a hook promises something specific ("the exact system I used," "the template," "the leaked secrets"), the lead magnet is the literal, deliverable version of that promise — not a generic opt-in unrelated to what made the post work in the first place.

This also enforces the honesty constraint the source raises elsewhere: a hook can't promise something untrue just because it's catchy — whatever the hook claims, the lead magnet has to actually deliver on it.

---

## 5. Build checklist for an AI content system

A practical sequence an AI system can follow to turn this into working assets:

- **Step 1 — Mine for the promise.** Look back through your highest-performing Growth and Authority hooks (per the Post Frameworks guide) for any hook that implies a deliverable: "the template," "the system," "the exact steps," "the leaked secrets," etc.
- **Step 2 — Build the literal deliverable.** Turn that exact promise into a real asset: a one-pager, a short video, a checklist, a swipe file, a worksheet. The asset's content should match the hook's specific claim word-for-word, not a watered-down general version.
- **Step 3 — Place it in three locations.** (a) Featured section on the profile, (b) the profile's custom CTA button, (c) the closing line of qualifying Conversion-bucket posts.
- **Step 4 — Choose a delivery mechanic.** Comment-to-receive (via DM), direct DM request ("DM me [keyword]"), or a link placed under the post / under your name rather than inline in the post body.
- **Step 5 — Sequence it after trust-building content.** Never lead with it. Schedule it to land after a run of Growth/Authority posts, consistent with the bucket ratio (see Post Frameworks guide, Section D).
- **Step 6 — Make sure it routes off-platform into something you own.** Newsletter, booking page, or download — anywhere LinkedIn doesn't own the relationship, per the explicit reasoning in Section 1.
