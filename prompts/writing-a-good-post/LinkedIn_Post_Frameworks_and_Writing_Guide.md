## A. The Hook — The Movie Preview Framework (Matthew Leave)

Matt's core idea: a hook is a movie preview. You're not trying to explain the post in the hook — you're trying to make someone want to see what happens, the same way a trailer makes you want to watch the film. Every hook that breaks through is doing four things at once.

### The 4 parts of a hook

1. **Cast, Setting, Genre** — familiar names, brands, places, or categories that the reader already recognizes. Recognition stops the scroll before the reader even processes meaning. Examples from the source: Diary of a CEO, Steven Bartlett, Ray Dalio; Bezos' wedding in Venice; an Indian wedding; Grok, ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini named together.
2. **Story** — the promise of what the reader will find out if they keep reading. This is the "why" that makes the "see more" click worth it.
3. **Conflict** — the tension, problem, or contradiction inside the post. No conflict, no reason to keep reading. The bigger and more unexpected the conflict, the harder the hook pulls.
4. **Director's Cut** — your own opinion, lived experience, or take. This is the part Google or ChatGPT cannot reproduce, because it only exists inside your head. It's also what actually builds the personal brand — the first three parts borrow attention, the fourth is what makes that attention convert into trust.

### The build-test for a hook

> "You can't go onto Google and... not even ChatGPT can tell you. It's too difficult." — Matthew Leave, on what makes a hook actually his own

Before publishing, run the hook against this question: could someone get this exact content by Googling it or asking an AI model? If yes, the Story and Director's Cut aren't sharp enough yet — keep rewriting.

### The rewriting process Matt actually uses

Matt doesn't write one hook and move on — he writes a first pass, then rewrites it five to ten times, each version adding more Cast/Setting/Genre recognition and sharper Conflict, while making numbers more specific (real figures, not "a lot" or "millions"). Example progression from the source material:

- "The fastest way to make a million dollars on LinkedIn with less than 1,000 followers and zero viral posts."
- "How I made a million dollars on LinkedIn in three months — step-by-step process so you can copy me."
- "How I stole Diary of a CEO's three tactics and made $1 million on LinkedIn in three months."
- "I found leaked Diary of a CEO internal memos on Reddit. It made me $1 million on LinkedIn in three months."

Notice the pattern: each rewrite adds a more specific Cast/Setting/Genre element (Diary of a CEO → leaked memos → Reddit) and raises the Conflict (secret → leaked → scraped). Build a hook the same way — write it, then ask "how do I make this more specific and more tense" on every pass.

### Niche theory: write for the weird indie movie, not the blockbuster

Matt's analogy: his wife loves psychological horror and hates gore; he loves epic fantasy and hates horror entirely. Neither of them will watch a film outside their genre no matter how well-made it is. Your content works the same way — it should be the hyper-specific "indie film" that your ICP is obsessed with, even if it means nothing to anyone else.

- **Beck's post (~1,700 followers):** "Same day, same industry, completely different decisions" — comparing two agriculture locations 300km apart. Outsiders couldn't parse it. Her exact ICP could. Result: 156 likes, 20 inbound leads.
- **Dan G's post:** a breakdown of how to analyze a commercial property in 20 minutes, built around an Excel sheet only people doing real-estate valuations would recognize. Result: 400 likes from a tiny, precise audience.

Takeaway: don't optimize a post to be understood by everyone. Optimize it to be unmistakably for your ICP.

### First two to three lines = the trailer before "see more"

LinkedIn cuts posts off behind a "...more" link after roughly the first two to three lines. Everything before that cutoff has to carry the full weight of Cast/Setting/Genre plus the start of the Conflict — it is the only part of the post guaranteed to be seen before the reader decides whether to click further.

## B. The 4 Growth Post Formats (Distinct Diva)

These exist to solve what the source calls the "authority paradox": the more expertise you have, the more you assume people will care — but attention and expertise are different currencies, and on LinkedIn, attention has to be earned first. Growth formats work by attaching your expertise to attention that already exists, instead of trying to create attention from zero.

### 1. Brandjacking

Take a well-known brand's decision, campaign, product, mistake, or pivot and use it as the frame for your own insight. The rule: you are not reporting on or summarizing the brand — you are borrowing the recognition their name already has and redirecting it toward a point that is genuinely yours.

- Hines dropping its logo from billboards while keeping 70% brand recognition → used to make a point about creative consistency vs. testing velocity in paid social. Result: 9,000 impressions, 193 reactions, 33 saves.
- Liquid Death's creative team generating billions in earned media without a traditional ad budget → 38,000 impressions. Liquid Death's VP of Creative commented to correct a detail; that single comment got 190 likes.

**The Boomerang Effect:** When the subject of your post (the brand or person) notices and engages, the algorithm reads that as a signal that something real is happening and pushes the post harder. You don't need to be famous to trigger this — you need to write something sharp enough about a brand's decision that the brand notices.

### 2. Newsjacking

Be the first credible voice to contextualize something significant happening in your industry — not summarize it, explain what it actually means and why the obvious read might be wrong.

- **Where to find news before it hits LinkedIn:** LinkedIn is consistently behind. Search Google directly for trends in your space daily; ask an LLM (ChatGPT or Claude) what's happening in your industry this week; watch X, which moves first; or build automations that surface breaking stories before anyone posts about them on LinkedIn.
- **Timing rule:** a sharp take posted within 24–48 hours of a story breaking gets distributed significantly harder than the same take published days later.
- **Readiness test:** can you answer "so what" in one sentence? If not, you're still summarizing — keep refining until you have an actual position.

Results cited: ChatGPT testing ads inside the chat window — 130 comments within days of the story breaking. ChatGPT pulling directly from Google search — 293 comments, 234 reposts, ~28,000–29,000 impressions. Bing's AI performance dashboard and what it means for search visibility — 70 reposts, ~40,500 impressions.

### 3. Namejacking

Reference a specific named person your audience already follows — someone you agree with, want to build on, or respectfully disagree with — to stop the scroll, then deliver a take that is entirely your own. It works like brandjacking but is more precise, because the person's followers usually already overlap with your ICP.

- Satya Nadella replacing "know-it-all" culture with "learn-it-all" culture at Microsoft → connected to hiring philosophy in growth-stage companies. ~23,000–24,000 impressions.
- Mira Murati turning down a billion-dollar acquisition offer from Zuckerberg → connected to what loyalty inside organizations actually looks like. ~12,000 impressions.
- A post referencing Steven Bartlett, connected to his business model — Bartlett himself commented on it. 147,000 impressions.

Rule: the moment you're just repeating what the person said, you've lost the point — namejacking only works with a genuine, added perspective, not a summary.

### 4. Hot Takes

A contrarian position on something your entire industry accepts as true — not provocation for its own sake, but a belief you actually hold, built from a pattern you've observed, that contradicts the industry consensus.

- **The nerves test:** does it make you slightly nervous to post? If you could post it with zero anxiety, it's probably not contrarian enough to move people.

Results cited: "Original research is outranking AI-written content — not by a little, by a lot, and the gap is widening" — ~390–396 comments, ~300–304 reposts, ~3,198 reactions. "ChatGPT sold its soul the moment it started testing ads" — 137 comments, ~72,000 impressions, still climbing days later.

### What all four formats share

Every one of them attaches your expertise to something that already has an audience's attention, rather than trying to build attention from a standing start. Recommended cadence: one to two growth-format posts per week, mixed in with regular authority content, to keep new-audience reach and existing-audience trust both moving.

## C. The How To → How I Shift (Distinct Diva)

The argument: "How to" content positions you as a teacher standing above the audience — and AI can now generate a personalized version of that instantly, for free, in seconds. "How I" content positions you as someone figuring it out alongside the audience, sharing real decisions and real results, which AI cannot replicate because it didn't happen to the AI.

### Five content types people still actively seek out (none are tutorials)

1. Long-form writing that connects ideas across topics in an original way.
2. Effortful work — content where it's visible someone put real time and skill into making it.
3. Demonstrations — showing the actual process, not just explaining the concept.
4. Interviews with people who have unique, verifiable experience.
5. "Good hangs" — creators who simply make the audience feel comfortable and at ease.

### Rewrite formula: How To → How I

| How To (generic, AI-replaceable) | How I (specific, only you can say it) |
|---|---|
| How to write a LinkedIn hook that stops the scroll | I tested 47 hooks last month. Here's what I learned about my audience. |
| The 5-step framework for client onboarding | We changed our onboarding process after losing a client. Here's what we do differently. |
| How to price your services | I raised my prices 40% and lost two clients. Here's why I'd do it again. |

### Why this matters strategically, not just stylistically

The source notes LinkedIn is reportedly the second most cited source inside AI overviews — meaning your posts aren't only competing for feed attention, they're also feeding the systems that will eventually recommend things to your future buyers. Generic how-to content gets absorbed and summarized, and your framework becomes "everyone's framework." "How I" content becomes a specific, attributable example tied to you — you become the case study, not the commodity.

### How to actually make the shift

- **Document, don't instruct.** Next time you solve a problem for a client or make a business decision, write about what you actually did, why you did it, and what happened — not what people "should" do.
- **Share learning in real time.** You don't need to have it figured out before you post — some of the best content comes from being mid-challenge.
- **Include the failures.** Wins are important, but losses are what make you relatable; the source calls this where "the trust actually gets built."
- **Show the actual work.** Screen-share, show the document, walk through the real example — let people see how you think, not just hear you describe it.

Note: the source is explicit that the how-to era "isn't completely dead" — very specific instructional content still has a place. The shift is about not relying on how-to as the only or primary content type.

## D. The 4-Bucket Content Funnel (Distinct Diva)

Every post should fall into one of four buckets, each doing a different job in moving someone from stranger to client. The source compares this to a sales team: one person brings leads, one builds trust, one closes, one keeps relationships warm. When every post does the same job, the whole pipeline breaks.

### The four buckets

- **Growth** — reach new people. Industry observations, trend commentary, pattern recognition, hot takes. Deliberately broad. The goal: someone who's never heard of you sees the post and thinks "who is this person?" Not designed to convert anyone.
- **Authority** — build trust. Frameworks, case studies, deep expertise, client results. Deliberately narrower than growth content — that's the point, you want the right people paying attention, not everyone. This is the difference between "interesting opinions" and "this person can actually help me."
- **Conversion** — actually ask for the business. Lead magnets, direct offers, CTAs that move people from engaged followers to qualified conversations. Only works once growth and authority content have already built trust — you can't convert people who don't trust you yet.
- **Personal** — show the human behind the expertise. Real story, perspective, behind-the-scenes. Deepens connection with people who already follow you and makes them remember you specifically, not just your advice. Only works once you've built a community — if nobody knows you yet, they won't care about personal updates. Explicitly not "what my dog taught me about B2B sales" — it has to be a real update your community is actually invested in.

### The ratio

The source material states two slightly different splits across two separate videos. Both agree the ratios are not hard rules — the only firm rule is that all four buckets should appear across a rolling month.

| Bucket | Version 1 (LinkedIn System for 2026 intro) | Version 2 (Best LinkedIn Content Strategy video) |
|---|---|---|
| Growth | 40% | 35% |
| Authority | 30% | 35% |
| Conversion | 20% | 20% |
| Personal | 10% | 10% |

### Important nuance: buckets are about intent, not style

A Growth post and an Authority post can both use storytelling, and both can educate. What separates them is the job each one is doing inside the funnel — not the format or tone used to do it.

## E. Rehooks — what they are and how they should work

The source uses "rehook" specifically to describe the profile banner's job inside the visitor funnel, and the same logic extends naturally to the moment right after the "see more" cutoff inside a single post.

### The documented definition: the banner as rehook

The funnel described in the source runs: your post is what stops the scroll → your headline gets the click → your banner needs to keep them moving down the page — this is the rehook. A good banner should make a visitor think: "I need to keep reading. I like this person. I want to know more," and should visually pull the eye toward the featured section, which is the next step in the funnel.

### Applying the same logic inside a post

The principle generalizes: a rehook's job is to sustain momentum the original hook already created, not to start a new pitch from scratch. Inside a single post, this shows up at the "see more" cutoff. LinkedIn's algorithm (360Brew, covered in the Additional System Notes doc) tracks dwell time and genuine read-through rather than just first-hour likes, so the line immediately after "...more" is functioning as a rehook — it needs to carry forward the same Cast/Setting/Genre and Conflict tension established in the hook, rather than slowing down to recap or re-explain. If that line drops the tension, the reader leaves and the post never accumulates the read-through signal the algorithm rewards.

Practical rule: write the line right after the fold as if it has to re-earn the click all over again — because it does.

## F. How a post should end — endings and CTAs by bucket

Step five of the 5-step writing process (Section G) is matching the closing line to the bucket the post belongs to:

- **Growth posts** — end with a question that sparks discussion, or no explicit CTA at all. The goal is the widest possible engagement, not a specific action.
- **Authority posts** — end with something actionable but soft: "DM me for the template," "comment X for the full framework." This converts attention into a specific, trackable action without asking for the sale.
- **Conversion posts** — end with the actual ask — a link (often placed under your name rather than in the post body, since LinkedIn suppresses outbound links), "DM me [keyword]," or a direct statement that you're opening availability. This is the one bucket where a direct CTA belongs.
- **Personal posts** — often need no CTA at all. The goal is connection, not action.

### After it's posted: the reply rule

Reply to every comment, especially within the first hour. More replies mean more distribution from the algorithm, but the source is explicit that the more important reason is relational: these are real people who took the time to respond, and it's how relationships with your audience actually form.

## G. Post length, formatting, and the 5-step writing process

### Formatting rules stated in the source

- Paragraphs: 2–3 lines max, written for mobile.
- Use white space generously — "let the post breathe."
- Write at a 7th–8th grade reading level — simple language, clear ideas.
- Read it out loud before posting — if you wouldn't say it in conversation, rewrite it.
- Formatting itself is "completely personal" — one client's post broke every formatting convention and still went viral. The only fixed goal is readability; the rules can flex by context.

### Visuals

Not mandatory on every post, but the source's internal data points toward including one on most posts — a visual makes a post more scannable, clearer, and adds credibility. Options mentioned: framework diagrams, screenshots, before/after data, photos of you speaking or working.

- Name carousel files descriptively before uploading — the system reads the filename as a relevance signal.
- Add alt text to images — it's read by the algorithm and almost nobody does it.

### The 5-step writing process

1. **Choose your intent.** Decide which bucket (Growth, Authority, Conversion, Personal) this post is for before writing anything — it shapes the hook, structure, and CTA that follow.
2. **Write the body first, not the hook.** Get the ideas, numbers, frameworks, and examples down first — the "word vomit" draft.
3. **Mine the body for your hook.** Read back through the draft and find the most interesting, surprising, or provocative line — move it to the top. A hook needs to do one of three things: create curiosity, challenge a belief, or promise specific value.
4. **Add a visual if it helps.** See above.
5. **End with a CTA that matches your bucket.** See Section F.

## H. Studying your niche before you post

Both sources converge on the same point from different angles: research before you publish.

### Distinct Diva's approach

Study accounts with strong engagement specifically from your ICP — not just large follower counts. A 3,000-follower account whose comments come from your exact ICP is more valuable to study than a 100,000-follower account whose comments are mostly other creators. Track what topics recur, what hooks stop the scroll, what formats get traction, how posts end, and — most importantly — what the outliers are and why.

### Matt's approach: the "Steal Like an Artist" method

Study many creators in your niche, absorb what resonates, and rebuild it around your own experience. Steal formats, hooks, and frameworks — never ideas or identity. After roughly six months of doing this consistently, the source notes you stop noticing what's working for everyone else and start noticing what's missing in your niche — that's the point original point-of-view content starts to emerge.

## I. The honest timeline

Stated identically across two separate videos in the source material — treat this as the realistic expectation-setting baseline:

| Post range | What's actually happening |
|---|---|
| Posts 1–20 | Practice. Don't expect results yet. |
| Posts 21–40 | You start finding your actual voice and early community. |
| Post 41+ | Momentum and content-market-fit start to compound. |

Minimum commitment recommended: 90 days, which works out to roughly 40–50 posts at a 3–5x/week cadence. Don't quit before post 41.
