# The LinkedIn Post Playbook
### Hooks, Frameworks, Rehooks & Endings — Compiled from Source Material

This playbook compiles every LinkedIn post-writing principle, framework, and structural rule found across the source documents, organized by structural component (hook → body → rehook → ending), and tagged by source. Built to be used as a reference doc or fed directly into an AI writing system as a style/structure guide.

---

## 1. The Core Shift in How LinkedIn Posts Should Be Written
**Source: LARA Strategies**

The old advice — a short hook line, a blank line, then another short line, repeated down the page — matters less than it used to. LinkedIn itself has said (at a recent creator event referenced in the source) that it is now prioritizing expert-led content over perfectly-curated, perfectly-templated content.

What that means practically:

- **Subject-matter expertise beats a polished template.** A post that opens with "I just came back from this expo and did this exact thing" outperforms a beautifully engineered 8-word hook, because it's packed with specific, expert keywords (e.g. "sales expo") instead of generic curiosity bait.
- **The first two lines are the real battlefield.** Whatever framework you use, the opening two lines need to make the reader instantly categorize you as a credible expert in a specific thing.
- **Don't over-engineer the format.** Frameworks (below) are for people who want a repeatable system, not a rigid law. Fill-in-the-blank is fine.

## 2. Hooks
**Source: LARA Strategies**

### What a hook needs to do
A hook is the first line (or two) of the post — its only job is to stop the scroll and signal expertise immediately.

- Target length: roughly **8 words**. This is a guideline, not a hard rule.
- It should be **keyword-dense, not just curiosity-dense** — name the specific topic, role, event, or result (e.g. "sales expo", "cold outreach") rather than a vague teaser.
- A slightly rougher, more "real" hook that proves you actually did the thing ("I just came back from this expo and did this exact thing") currently **outperforms** an over-polished, perfectly-crafted hook — LinkedIn is rewarding authenticity/expertise signals over copywriting polish right now.

### Hook formula by use case

| Use case | Hook approach | Example pattern |
|---|---|---|
| Conversion / advice post (PASS) | State the problem your ICP has, directly, in their language | "If you're [role] and you're not doing [specific thing], you're losing [specific result]." |
| Story / educational post (SLAY) | Open mid-action with a specific, recent moment | "I just came back from [specific event] and I learned [specific thing]." |
| Expert / authority post | Lead with your specific niche + recent proof, no fluff | "[Role] + [specific topic] + [what just happened]" |

## 3. Writing Frameworks: PASS & SLAY
**Source: LARA Strategies**

These are the two repeatable writing frameworks in the source material. Pick the framework based on the **goal** of the post, not the topic.

### PASS — for conversion / direct-advice posts
*Use PASS when your goal is for the reader to take an action (try a tactic, rethink something, get convinced).*

| Letter | Stands for | What goes there |
|---|---|---|
| P | Problem | Name a specific problem your ICP has. ("If you were this exact salesperson, you'd be losing out on leads if you're not using SEO.") |
| A | Agitate | Sharpen the cost of the problem / hint at the fix coming. ("Here's exactly how to fix it.") |
| S | Solution | Outline the solution step-by-step. Keep it simple — don't over-complicate. |
| S | + Rehook | Close with a rehook that loops back to the opening hook (see Section 4). |

### SLAY — for storytelling / educational posts
*Use SLAY when your goal is to build trust, emotion, and relatability through a real story.*

| Letter | Stands for | What goes there |
|---|---|---|
| S | Story | Tell the specific story. ("I just came back from this event and I learned this thing.") |
| L | Lesson | What you actually learned / the insight extracted from the story. |
| A | Action | An actionable piece of advice the reader can use — the "what's in it for me" payoff. |
| Y | You | Turn it back to the reader — "what about you?" / invite them into the story (this doubles as engagement bait). |

Both frameworks work because they build emotion and trust fast, with no corporate jargon — just clear, direct writing. The recommendation in the source: have these two frameworks ready as templates so that whenever you see a piece of "winning content" in your niche, you can immediately re-write it into your own words using one of these two shapes.

## 4. Rehooks — What They Are & How They Work
**Source: LARA Strategies**

A **rehook** is a closing line (or short section) at the end of a post that loops back to the opening hook — it re-states or re-frames the original hook so the post feels like a closed loop instead of trailing off.

### How a rehook functions structurally

- **In PASS:** the rehook sits at the very end, after the Solution. It reconnects the reader to the original problem stated in the hook — e.g. opening with "you're losing out on leads if you're not using SEO" and rehooking with something like "that's the SEO gap that's quietly costing you leads" — so the structure is **Hook → Problem → Agitate → Solution → Rehook**.
- **In SLAY:** the "You" step functions as a rehook — it takes the personal story from the opening and redirects it at the reader ("this is exactly how I did it... and what about you?"), closing the loop between "my story" and "your situation."

### Why it matters

- It gives the post a sense of completeness, which keeps readers from bouncing mid-scroll.
- It re-exposes the core keyword/topic of the hook a second time, reinforcing what the post is actually about (useful for both human re-readers and the algorithm's topic-relevance signals).
- It naturally creates a transition into a question or call-to-action, since the loop is now "closed" and the reader is primed to respond.

## 5. How a Post Should End — Endings & CTAs
**Source: LARA Strategies (LinkedIn) + Ayman Strategies (adapted structural principle)**

The source material doesn't treat the ending as an afterthought — it's the highest-leverage few words in the post, because the people who read to the end are the most engaged and the most likely to act.

### Ending mechanics that drive engagement

- **Close the loop with a rehook** (see Section 4) before any CTA — don't jump straight from your last point to an ask.
- **Use the "You" turn.** In SLAY, ending on a direct question back to the reader ("what about you?") is itself a built-in engagement mechanism — it gives people an easy, low-friction reason to comment.
- **Keep the actual CTA short and concrete.** The video-script source (Ayman Strategies) makes the same point in a different format (YouTube outros): the close should be done "word for word," concise and simple — literally telling the audience what to do next, not a vague "let me know your thoughts." Applied to LinkedIn: end with **one clear, specific ask** (comment a word, share an experience, click one link) — not three asks at once.
- **Add a transitionary line before the ask.** Ayman Strategies stresses that abruptly jumping from your last content point into the outro/CTA causes a sharp drop-off (visible in YouTube retention graphs; the LinkedIn equivalent is people bouncing before they comment). A short bridge sentence ("so that's exactly how I did this") smooths the transition into the ending.
- **The post's job is relationship, not the sale.** LARA Strategies is explicit: LinkedIn is a relationship platform — people buy from people they know, like, and trust. The content's job is to build that relationship, not to close a sale directly in the comments. Save direct offers for lead-magnet posts and your email list (see the separate Lead Magnet document).

### Ending options by post type

| Post type | Best ending | Why |
|---|---|---|
| PASS (advice) | Rehook restating the problem/solution, optional single CTA | Reinforces the takeaway; keeps it concrete and actionable |
| SLAY (story) | "And what about you?" direct question | Built-in comment bait; invites the reader into the narrative |
| Lead magnet post | Single, explicit instruction (comment a keyword / click the link) | Mirrors Ayman's outro rule: concise, one ask, word-for-word clear |
| Educational / case study | A one-line summary + open question about their own experience | Encourages "me too / not me" style comments which drive reach |

## 6. Post Length
**Source: LARA Strategies**

The source material is explicit that the old format-driven length rule (short hook, blank line, short next line, repeat) is no longer the deciding factor for performance. There's no fixed word-count given — instead, length is governed by these rules:

- **Length should match the framework, not a template.** PASS posts can be short and punchy (problem → agitate → solution can all be done in a handful of lines). SLAY posts need enough room to actually tell a story with a beginning, a lesson, and a turn to the reader — so they'll typically run longer.
- **The first two lines matter more than total length.** Whatever the eventual length, the opening two lines are what decide if the post gets read at all — that's where the expertise signal has to land.
- **Quality over quantity applies to frequency, not just length.** The source recommends 3–4 high-quality posts per week over higher daily volume — "three or four pieces of quality content, that's all you need" — rather than chasing 5–10 posts/week.
- **Always pair the post with an image.** Every high-performing post referenced in the source has an image attached. LinkedIn is described as a visual platform even though it's text-first — an image is treated as close to mandatory for growth.

## 7. Post Types / Content Themes
**Source: LARA Strategies**

One underlying system or idea can be turned into 5 different content types. Treat these as your content menu — rotate through them rather than reinventing a new idea every week.

| Content type | Purpose | Typical framework |
|---|---|---|
| Educational | Teach a system/process step-by-step; safest bet for credibility and leads | PASS |
| Case study | Show how you implemented the system for yourself or a client | PASS or SLAY |
| Story | Tell the story of how you found/built the system; builds relatability and trust | SLAY |
| Lead magnet post | Offer a free resource tied to the system, captures email | PASS (with explicit CTA) — see Lead Magnet document |
| Sales post | Directly offer the paid version / your service | PASS |

### The 4-3-2-1 content framework

- **4** = post 4x per week to reach "escape velocity."
- **3** = rotate through 3 content types: Educational, Storytelling, and TAM (Total Addressable Market) content — i.e. reacting to current news/trends in your niche in your own voice.
- **2** = speak to 2 audience types at all times (e.g. people who could become clients, and people who could become referrers/community).
- **1** = there is one key thing that converts attention into sales off the back of a personal brand — the lead magnet → email system (covered fully in the separate Lead Magnet document).

Example weekly cadence from the source (agency-owner example):

1. **Monday** — Educational: step-by-step on a strategy (e.g. "how to get more leads using cold outreach").
2. **Wednesday** — Storytelling: a personal story tied to the business (e.g. "how I work remotely while scaling to $10k/month").
3. **Thursday** — TAM/news-jack: react to current news in your niche in your own words (e.g. a tool update and how your audience can use it) — being early and giving your specific point of view builds authority fast.
4. **Sunday** — Flex slot: repeat one of the above types, or post a one-off story/update.

### Quality vs. quantity

- LinkedIn is described as a quality game, not a quantity game right now — going from 0 to 100k+ followers was done on 3–4 quality posts/week, not daily volume.
- Within that, an **80/20 split** is recommended between Educational and Storytelling content (in either direction — 80% educational/20% story, or flip it — whichever resonates more with your specific audience, tested over time).

## 8. Visuals & Signature Style
**Source: LARA Strategies**

- **Always attach an image.** Every top-performing post example in the source has one — people are visual even on a text platform like LinkedIn.
- **Build a signature style / series.** Find a format that performs (e.g. a recurring breakdown style, a recurring carousel format) and repeat it deliberately until it stops working, then evolve it. Repetition builds brand recognition and removes the "what do I post today" decision fatigue.
- **Treat every post as a data point.** Track what format/style/length/vocabulary gets the best response, and iterate — this is described as a data approach, not a vibes approach.

## 9. The Engagement Layer That Powers a Post
**Source: LARA Strategies**

A well-written post underperforms without an engagement strategy around it. The source is explicit that this is the single biggest growth lever, bigger than the writing itself.

- Spend roughly an hour a day engaging — ideally 20 minutes before your own post goes live (commenting on your feed, industry peers, and replying to yesterday's comments).
- Aim for at least **30 genuine comments per day** on other people's posts.
- Reply to every single comment on your own post, ideally within the **first 30 minutes** of posting, and keep replying "until you can't handle it."
- Treat every comment like a mini-post: never write "great post" or a thumbs-up emoji. Add a story, a relatable detail, or a joke — something with actual value.
- If you can't be present to engage on a given day, the recommendation is: **don't post that day at all**, since LinkedIn distribution depends on early network engagement.

Manufactured engagement (in the genuine, human sense above — not bots/AI comments) signals to LinkedIn that the post is good, which pushes it further into non-followers' feeds and compounds growth over time.

## 10. Quick-Build Template (Fill-in-the-Blank)

For an AI system or a human writer to use directly when generating a post.

### PASS template
1. **Hook** (≈ 8 words, keyword-rich): "If you're [role] and [specific situation], [specific cost]."
2. **Problem:** Name the specific problem in the reader's language.
3. **Agitate:** "Here's exactly how to fix it."
4. **Solution:** Step-by-step fix, plain language, no jargon.
5. **Rehook:** Loop back to the opening problem/keyword.
6. **Ending:** One clear, single CTA or one direct question.

### SLAY template
1. **Hook:** "I just [specific recent action/event] and I [specific result/lesson]."
2. **Story:** Tell the specific moment — what happened, in order.
3. **Lesson:** What you actually learned.
4. **Action:** One concrete, usable takeaway for the reader.
5. **You:** "...and what about you?" — direct question to invite comments.
6. *(Attach one image. Post 3–4x/week. Engage for ~1 hour around publish time.)*
