# LinkedIn System — Additional Notes

*Everything from the source transcripts that didn't fit into the Post Frameworks guide or the Lead Magnets guide: the algorithm, profile/SEO setup, infrastructure, engagement mechanics, common mistakes, and pre-work assets. All from the LinkedIn System for 2026 material (Distinct Diva).*

## 1. How the algorithm actually works (360Brew)

LinkedIn published a research paper describing a rebuilt recommendation system, internally called 360Brew, that replaced the old approach entirely.

### Old system vs. new system

| Old system | New system (360Brew) |
|---|---|
| A "committee" of separate modules: one tracked trending topics, one tracked hashtags, one measured how fast a post got likes in the first hour | A single AI model, ~150 billion parameters, built in-house and trained entirely on LinkedIn's own data — "one brain, not a committee" |
| Matched keywords | Reads meaning — asks whether a post belongs in front of a specific person based on everything it knows about them |
| Tracked whether you posted at the "optimal time" | Tracks whether people stopped and actually read what you wrote (dwell time) |
| Could be gamed with engagement pods | Distinguishes a real comment from someone typing "great insight" without reading |

### It favors smaller accounts now

LinkedIn's own A/B testing before rollout showed measurably better outcomes for smaller accounts under the new system. Cited data from 50,000 posts published in late 2025:

- A top-10% post from a 5,000–10,000-follower account: ~5,500 impressions.
- An average/medium post from a 25,000–50,000-follower account: ~2,400–4,000 impressions.

In other words: a great post from a smaller account can outperform an average post from an account five times its size. This is why execution now matters more than audience size, and why the source argues now — after the post-2022 boom flattened out — is a good time to start posting.

### "Total Addressable Content" (TAC)

The same way a business has a Total Addressable Market, your content has a Total Addressable Audience. The practical question: what is that audience already watching, reading, and reacting to right now? That's your entry point — it's the underlying logic behind the four growth formats in the Post Frameworks guide (brandjacking, newsjacking, namejacking, hot takes).

### What stopped working / what's rewarded now

| No longer works | Now rewarded |
|---|---|
| Timing optimization | Genuine expertise |
| Hashtags | Content people actually dwell on |
| Engagement pods | Saves over likes |
| Generic advice everyone is posting | A voice that sounds like a real human with real expertise |

## 2. LinkedIn SEO — the most ignored growth lever

The algorithm reads your profile as a context signal before it distributes any single post — effectively asking "based on who this person says they are, does this post belong in front of this audience?"

### Where to put keywords

- **Headline:** use keywords, not a job title. Cited example: instead of "SEO consultant helping B2B SaaS teams grow through organic and AI search," use "SEO and AEO consultant for B2B SaaS" — because it matches what buyers actually search for, including the newer AEO (AI/answer-engine optimization) terminology.
- **Services section:** described as "searchable gold" that most people skip entirely. Fill it with the exact language your ICP uses when describing what they need.
- **About section:** write it as a sales page using natural keywords — not stuffed, not forced — phrased the way your buyer would describe their own problem out loud.
- **Posting consistency:** the system builds a "content fingerprint" of you over time. Posting about the same core keywords every week trains the algorithm to route that topic's content toward your audience automatically.
- **File and image details:** name carousel files descriptively before uploading (the system reads filenames), and add alt text to images — almost nobody does this and it is read by the system.

### Proof case cited

An SEO consultant who'd watched impressions flatline for six months changed only his headline and About section — same posting style, same content, same frequency — and saw reach up 226% and engagement up 327%. A prospect found him through LinkedIn search the same week.

## 3. Profile as a landing page (the funnel)

Core idea: your content is the ad, your profile is the landing page. If the ad is great but the landing page is confusing, nobody buys. The sequence:

- Post and headline get a stranger to click through to your profile.
- Banner (the rehook — see Post Frameworks guide, Section E) keeps them moving down the page.
- About section builds the case (problem, solution, proof, CTA — not a life story).
- Featured section and the custom CTA button pull them into your owned world (see Lead Magnets guide).

When any one step is unclear, the visitor leaves — the source recommends spending one full day on this before writing a single post.

### Headline structure

Not a job title — a value proposition: the problem you solve, who you solve it for, in the exact language buyers search for. Cited example structure: "Building content engines. LinkedIn newsletters, YouTube founder, [company]. We grow B2B brands through organic content. Content strategist, speaker."

### About section structure

One structure: problem, solution, proof, CTA. Show you understand the pain, position yourself as the fix, add numbers, then tell people exactly what to do next — not an autobiography.

## 4. Infrastructure stack

The source's stated position: the best content strategy in the world fails without infrastructure, because without it you're relying on motivation instead of a repeatable process.

### Notion content calendar (Kanban)

Columns used, in order:

- **Ideas** — raw, half-formed thoughts: a hook from a walk, a framework explained on a client call, a topic to tackle eventually. Nothing polished, just captured.
- **In progress** — actively writing, turning raw ideas into actual posts.
- **Design** — copy is done, now needs visuals (carousel, one-pager, image).
- **Needs edits** — flagged for a design or content edit (relevant if working with a team).
- **Posted** — every published post, dated and tagged by format.
- **Repurpose column (optional)** — posts that can become templates/frameworks for other team members to adapt — the source calls this an early step toward employee advocacy.

Every card is tagged by format (carousel, one-pager, video, image, text post) so a specific content type can be filtered instantly when needed. The stated benefit: you're never starting from zero — you're picking from a bank of ideas already waiting.

### Google Drive connected to your AI writing tool

Source material kept in Drive: meeting transcripts, SOPs, conference presentations, client onboarding docs, strategy decks. Connecting Drive to an AI tool (the source specifically recommends Claude) means you're never starting from a blank page — you can pull a sales call transcript and ask the AI to turn an objection-handling moment into a post, or extract several post ideas from a conference deck. The content already exists; Drive plus an AI connection just makes it usable.

### Claude project setup recommended for writing posts

Instructions section should include:

- Claude's role in the project.
- Your identity — who you are, what you do, who you help, your original story.
- Your opinions — spicy takes, contrarian beliefs, things you dislike in your industry.
- Writing rules — banned words (cited examples: "leverage," "unlock," "game changer," "dive deep"), formatting (no em dashes, one-to-three-line paragraphs, line breaks between paragraphs), and tone (conversational, use contractions).

Knowledge base section should include: a handful of your best existing LinkedIn posts (for writing-style matching), call transcripts and client conversations if available (or a Drive connection if not), case studies, frameworks you use, and — explicitly recommended — a folder of posts you dislike or "AI slop" examples, so the model also learns what to avoid.

## 5. The comment flywheel — engagement mechanics

In the first 90 days especially, comments matter more than your own posts. Posting to zero followers gets almost no visibility; commenting on a post with strong reach borrows distribution you haven't earned yet.

### What makes a comment count

A good comment does one of three things:

- Adds a new angle the original post didn't cover.
- Respectfully challenges the take.
- Tells a quick, relevant story connected to the topic.

"Great post" is explicitly called out as not a comment — it does nothing for you.

### Where to comment

Be strategic, not viral-chasing: find posts from people whose audience matches your ICP, not just the highest like-count. A post with 500 likes from the right people is worth more than one with 50,000 likes from the wrong ones.

### Building an engagement list

A simple spreadsheet with columns: category, the person's name, and their activity URL (not their profile URL). This matters specifically because: if you save someone's profile URL and visit it daily, and they have profile-view visibility turned on, they will see you looking at their profile every single day, which reads as stalking. The activity URL avoids this — found by going to their profile, selecting "show all posts," and copying that link instead.

Recommended time investment: 15–30 minutes a day on strategic comments, done before opening your own drafts, sustained for at least 90 days.

### Engaging on your own posts

Reply to every comment, especially in the first hour — more replies mean more algorithmic distribution, but the source frames the bigger reason as relational: these are real people who took the time to respond to you.

## 6. Three mistakes that kill results

1. **Optimizing for the wrong metrics.** Chasing impressions and followers instead of qualified leads. The source notes posts at 5,000 impressions have generated more qualified leads than posts at 100,000 — reach without relevance to your actual buyer is worthless.
2. **Posting without a funnel.** Posting whatever comes to mind with no bucket logic connecting the pieces — some posts should bring people in, some build trust, some convert; without that structure you're leaving outcomes on the table.
3. **No infrastructure.** Treating content strategy as just "the posts" or a vague idea about the business, rather than the system that actually produces the posts — this is what causes most people to burn out after a few weeks, because every sitting-down-to-write moment depends on motivation instead of process.

## 7. Pre-work assets recommended before posting

Two things the source says to do before building anything else:

### Research first

Identify the key players in your industry already posting on LinkedIn, study their outliers and what performs for them, and check whether your ICP tends to lurk or post. Skipping this step means starting in what the source calls "a really rough patch."

### Three documents to build

1. **Positioning document** — your ICP's problem and your solution; your primary positioning plus an alternative framing and why it works; style inspirations (the creators you researched); what to avoid; what your company specifically solves; your ICP's industry, geography, company size, and pain points; and a section on where your source content comes from (so it can connect to Drive and other tools for maximum context).
2. **Content north stars** — your guiding principles. Every piece of content should align with at least one, ideally several, of these. The stated risk of skipping this: without a clear direction, content-market-fit becomes extremely hard to find and the audience gets confused about what you're actually about.
3. **Tone and format guideline** — feeds directly into your AI writing tool so it can match your specific writing style. The source notes output quality scales with how much real source material you're able to upload.
